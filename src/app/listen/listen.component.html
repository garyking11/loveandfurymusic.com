ANNOUNCING A BENEFIT FOR UKRAINE / NEW SONG
BY SEATTLE BAND, LOVE AND FURY: “Berehynia Saves”

Seattle band, Love and Fury has created a new song called “Berehynia Saves.”With this song, we are inviting you to help us support the Ukrainian people who are enduring an unimaginable hellscape since the brutal and unlawful invasion of their sovereign nation by Vladimir Putin and The Russian Federation.

“Why do we do this to ourselves?” is a question asked by the Latvian poet, James Stillion, who Gary King asked to write a poem about the war atrocities in Ukraine. Gary and Gretta Harley used the poem to write the song, “Berehynia Saves.” Stillion’s question is a poignant testament to the complexities of human nature, where some wield power callously, disregarding the sanctity of human life. With our new song, Love and Fury hope to continue our mission to add an iota of something positive to these days of manufactured chaos. And now specifically to try to help the Ukrainian people who suffer so, using what we know how to do: make music.

Here’s where we hope you come in. We are asking for your donation of $5 for the the song. We have chosen to send proceeds to Save Ukraine.

Ukrainian children have been sent to re-education facilities inside Russia or the Russian-held areas of Ukraine under the pretext of "recovery" Children of War. Save Ukraine successfully conducts Rescue Missions to return stolen Ukrainian children to their homes.

Here’s a little bit about Berehynia:

Berehynia was believed to be the source of life and death. She is a life-giving mother/goddess, the creator of heaven and all living things, and the mistress of heavenly water (rain), upon which the world relies for fertility and fruitfulness.

Since Ukrainian independence in 1991 Berehynia has undergone a folkloric metamorphosis, and is today identified as a combination of the “hearth-mother” (associated with the guardianship of the nation) … Berehynia (represents) a place in Ukrainian nationalism, feminism, and neopaganism. The… interpretation as a “protectress” … a folk-etymology … is derived from the Ukrainian word bereh “river bank”, with the unrelated verb, berehty in Ukrainian which means “to protect”.
